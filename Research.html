1) Environmental change, population dynamics and community stability

Understanding how environmental change reshapes population dynamics and community stability is central to my research. Natural systems are increasingly exposed to warming, extreme events, and environmental variability, all of which can alter species interactions and destabilize ecological communities. I focus on how these drivers influence trophic interactions, particularly in predator–prey and host–parasitoid systems, where small changes can cascade into large population fluctuations. By integrating experimental and theoretical approaches, I aim to uncover the mechanisms that determine whether populations remain stable or exhibit oscillations, collapse, or recovery under changing environmental conditions.

2) Life-history selection under fluctuating environments

Environmental variability is a key driver of life-history strategy, yet its effects are often complex and context-dependent. My research explores how fluctuating environments shape life-history strategies, including development, survival, and reproduction, across interacting species. Rather than responding to average conditions, organisms must cope with variability, extremes, and the timing of stress events. I am particularly interested in how these factors generate stage-specific and species-specific responses, and how repeated exposure to environmental fluctuations can drive adaptive change. This work highlights the importance of variability—not just mean change—in shaping ecological and evolutionary dynamics.

3) Corridor-mediated dispersal and metapopulation persistence

Habitat loss and fragmentation is a major threat to biodiversity, and understanding how landscape connectivity influences population persistence is a key challenge. This work examines how habitat corridors mediate dispersal and shape metapopulation dynamics in fragmented landscapes. Corridors can facilitate movement between habitat patches, but their effectiveness depends on their quality, structure, and ecological context. I focus on how corridor-mediated dispersal influences extinction risk, population stability, and trait dynamics. This research aims to move beyond the simple question of whether corridors work, and instead understand the conditions under which they enhance persistence and resilience in spatially structured populations.

4) Landscape structure and pollination success

Ainimal-mediate pollination is a critical ecosystem service threatened by land use change. My research investigates how spatial configuration and landscape structure affect plant–pollinator interactions and pollination success. I am interested in how landscape features, such as corridors and linear elements, can mitigate these effects by enhancing connectivity. By linking landscape structure to ecological function, this work aims to provide a mechanistic understanding of how environmental change impacts pollination and to inform strategies for maintaining ecosystem services.
